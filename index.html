The Science of Stability: How Nuclear Power Works

What is a Nuclear Power Plant?

A facility that generates electricity using heat released from nuclear fission. Unlike other power plants that burn fuel, nuclear plants split uranium atoms in a controlled chain reaction to produce heat.

üè≠ A nuclear power plant consists of three key sections:

  • Nuclear Island - Contains the reactor, primary cooling system, and safety systems (unique to nuclear)
  • Turbine Island - Houses the steam turbines and generator (similar to fossil fuel plants)
  • Balance of Plant - Includes cooling systems, electrical switchyards, and support systems
Beyond the nuclear island, the process resembles conventional power plants - all ultimately use heat to create steam that drives turbines and generators.

How it Works

‚öõÔ∏è Nuclear Fission: Power from Splitting Atoms

Inside a nuclear reactor, uranium fuel pellets are packed into metal rods within the reactor core. When a neutron strikes a uranium-235 atom, the nucleus splits apart in a process called fission. This releases a burst of heat energy and additional neutrons, which trigger more fissions, creating a self-sustaining chain reaction. It's like a nuclear domino effect, powered by physics.

‚ô®Ô∏è Primary Circuit: Containing the Heat

The heat from fission is absorbed by water or another coolant flowing through the primary loop. This coolant is kept under high pressure, allowing it to stay extremely hot without boiling, much like a supercharged pressure cooker. Meanwhile, control rods made of neutron-absorbing materials (such as boron or cadmium) adjust the reaction rate, acting like a precision dial for nuclear energy.

üí® Secondary Circuit: Steam Power in Motion

The hot coolant passes through a steam generator, transferring its heat to a separate water system in the secondary loop. This water turns into high-pressure steam without ever mixing with the radioactive primary coolant.

‚ö° Electricity Generation: From Steam to Power

The steam rushes through massive turbines, spinning them at incredible speeds. These turbines drive a generator, converting mechanical energy into the electricity that powers homes, industries, and technology. It's a brilliant fusion of physics and engineering.

‚ùÑÔ∏è Cooling System: Completing the Cycle

After passing through the turbines, the steam is cooled back into liquid water in a condenser. This cooling often relies on water from rivers, oceans, or the iconic cooling towers that release plumes of vapor. The water is then recycled back into the system, because in nuclear energy, efficiency and sustainability matter.

Vision 2030, 2034: Kenya's Strategic Path to Nuclear Energy

Kenya's decision to explore nuclear energy was not sudden but a carefully considered move towards securing its long-term energy needs. The conversation formally began around 2010 when the National Economic and Social Council (NESC), Kenya's highest advisory body on government policies, recommended adopting a nuclear power program as a national priority. This recommendation stemmed from an escalating demand for electricity to fuel the country's development agenda, outlined in its Vision 2030.

The main drivers behind this strategic shift included:

  • Increasing Energy Demand: Kenya's economy is growing, and with it, the demand for electricity. The country has seen historic peaks in electricity demand, reaching 2,362.28 MW in July 2025, driven by new customer connections and increased industrial and commercial usage. Kenya Power projects even higher figures in the future, with annual demand potentially reaching 36,291 GWh by 2042.
  • Security of Supply: While Kenya boasts a remarkably low-carbon electricity grid; with nearly 90% of its power from clean sources like geothermal, hydropower, and wind; it faces vulnerabilities. Hydropower, for instance, is susceptible to climate variations like droughts, which can reduce output. Nuclear power offers a stable, continuous "baseload" source of energy that can run day and night, providing crucial reliability for large-scale manufacturing and infrastructure development, vital for Vision 2030's targets.
  • Economic Considerations: Nuclear energy is seen as a lower-cost alternative to fossil fuels in the long run and has the potential to reduce power costs, making Kenyan industries more competitive.
  • Environmental Concerns: As a clean energy source, nuclear power aligns with Kenya's commitment to reducing carbon emissions, contributing to a greener energy mix.

In 2010, a Committee on Nuclear Energy was formed within the Ministry of Energy to explore the feasibility of generating electricity from nuclear resources. This committee was later elevated to a Board, the Kenya Nuclear Electricity Board (KNEB), in 2012, tasked with fast-tracking Kenya's nuclear power program. In 2019, KNEB transitioned into the Nuclear Power and Energy Agency (NuPEA).

Recent Developments and Milestones

Kenya is progressing through Phase 2 of its nuclear energy program, guided by the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) Milestone Approach. This phased approach helps countries considering nuclear power to establish the necessary infrastructure, legal frameworks, and human resources. Key developments include:

  • Vision 2030 Targets: Kenya aims for a Research Reactor by 2030 and its first Nuclear Power Plant (NPP) by 2034. Construction of the 1,000 MW nuclear plant is set to begin in 2027 and begin operations by 2034. The project is estimated to cost in excess of Sh500 billion.
  • Regulatory and Legislative Framework: Kenya has established a robust governance framework aligned with international standards. The Kenya Nuclear Regulatory Authority (KNRA), formed in 2020, is the independent regulatory body responsible for radiation protection, nuclear safety, security, safeguards, and licensing. This ensures transparency and avoids conflicts of interest.
  • International Collaboration: Kenya actively collaborates with international partners like the IAEA, which provides guidance and conducts Integrated Nuclear Infrastructure Review (INIR) missions to assess the country's progress. Kenya has also signed cooperation agreements with the United States and China, and is awaiting another with South Korea, to support aspects of the nuclear project.
  • Human Resource Development: Recognizing the need for specialized expertise, Kenya has invested in training programs, sending students to study nuclear engineering abroad, notably at Korea Electric Power Company's KEPCO International Nuclear Graduate School (KINGS) and Tsinghua University Nuclear Engineering and Management (TUNEM) in China. Closer home, the recent opening of Kenya Advanced Institute of Science and Technology (Kenya-AIST), offering masters and PhD courses in Nuclear engineering. The University of Nairobi also offers a Master of Science in Nuclear Science & Technology at its Institute of Nuclear Science and Technology within the Faculty of Engineering, aimed at promoting peaceful applications of nuclear science. Jaramogi Oginga Odinga University of Science and Technology (JOOUST) is also set to become a training hub for the plant's workforce.
  • Environmental and Social Assessments: A Strategic Environmental and Social Assessment (SESA) is in place to ensure sustainable use of natural resources and addresses environmental and socio-economic factors.

A significant recent development is the potential relocation of the nuclear power plant project to Siaya County following strong opposition from residents in Kilifi County, where it was initially intended to be set up. The proposed new site is Lwanda Kotieno in Rarieda Constituency, Siaya, chosen partly due to its proximity to Lake Victoria, which is crucial for cooling nuclear reactors.

NuPEA has been actively involved in engaging stakeholders in Siaya. High-level forums have been held, where national and county leaders, including Siaya Governor James Orengo, local MPs, and senators, have largely welcomed the plan, viewing it as a transformative opportunity for the region. Energy Cabinet Secretary Opiyo Wandayi has emphasized the ministry's commitment to transparency, safety, and public participation in every stage of the project. While leaders have thrown their weight behind the project, they have also stressed the need for thorough public sensitization, simplified technical communication, and tangible benefits for the host communities, such as scholarships for local youth to become nuclear scientists and engineers.

The shift to Siaya underscores the government's commitment to public participation and finding a suitable location that garners local support, as resistance in Kilifi had cited concerns over radiation risks, water pollution, and perceived lack of honest engagement. NuPEA continues to work on identifying the most suitable locations around Lake Victoria for further scientific and environmental studies to determine the safest and most viable site.
